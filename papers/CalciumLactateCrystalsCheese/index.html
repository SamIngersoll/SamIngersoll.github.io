Revisiting Calcium Lactate Crystals in Cheese

M. Johnson. (2004). Revisiting Calcium Lactate Crystals in Cheese. Wisconsin Center for Dairy Research Dairy Pipeline, Volume 16 Number 1.

What problem does the paper solve and why is this interesting?

Johnson addresses the issue of the formation of calcium lactate crystals in cheddar cheese during storage. These crystals have been deemed undesirable reduce the percieved quality of the cheese.

How do they solve this problem?

Johnson investigates the cause of crystal formation, considering storage temperature -which appears to be correlated with crystal formation- but concludes that instead it was the mobility of fluids (called free serum) that was responsible for the formation of crystals on the surface of the cheese.
Johnson perscribes several heuristics for combating crystal formation including: correct, sealed packaging, avoiding temperature cycling and moisture migration especially to the surface of the cheese.
The factor that inevitably dictates whether calcium lactate crystals will form is the calcium lactate level in the serum phase of the cheese.

Future work and questions

Although methods exist to lower the lactic acid levels in the cheese -such as curd rinsing and using milk with lower levels of lactic acid- little is known about the exact processes which control the nucleation and proliferation of the crystals.
Sharp cheddars are sharp because of their lactic acid levels, produced by the metabolism of lactose during the extended aging period.
